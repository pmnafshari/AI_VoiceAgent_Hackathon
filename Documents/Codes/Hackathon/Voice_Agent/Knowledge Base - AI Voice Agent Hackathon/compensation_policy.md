# SkyItalia Airlines — Passenger Compensation and Rights Policy

## Regulatory Framework and Document Purpose

This document sets out SkyItalia Airlines' comprehensive passenger compensation policy, which is governed by **Regulation (EC) No 261/2004** of the European Parliament and of the Council of 11 February 2004 establishing common rules on compensation and assistance to passengers in the event of denied boarding and of cancellation or long delay of flights. The Regulation applies to all flights departing from an EU/EEA airport (regardless of the airline's nationality) and to flights arriving in the EU/EEA when operated by an EU/EEA carrier. SkyItalia, as an Italian-registered carrier with its principal place of business in Milano Malpensa (MXP), falls under the full scope of this Regulation for all routes in its network.

This policy document is maintained by the SkyItalia Legal and Customer Relations Department and is reviewed quarterly. The current version reflects the latest CJEU (Court of Justice of the European Union) case law through Q4 2025, including landmark rulings such as *Sturgeon v Condor* (C-402/07, establishing the 3-hour delay threshold for compensation), *Nelson v Lufthansa* (C-581/10, confirming the validity of the Sturgeon delay compensation principle), *Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia* (C-549/07, defining extraordinary circumstances), and *van der Lans v KLM* (C-257/14, clarifying technical fault exceptions).

SkyItalia's internal operations code for this policy is **POL-CMP-2024-003**. All customer service agents, ground handling staff, and operations controllers must be familiar with the contents of this document. Failure to correctly apply the compensation rules may result in regulatory sanctions from ENAC (Ente Nazionale per l'Aviazione Civile), Italy's national enforcement body, and reputational damage to the airline.

## 1. Scope and Applicability

### 1.1 Flights Covered

This policy applies to:

- All scheduled SkyItalia flights departing from any airport within the European Union, Iceland, Norway, Switzerland, or Liechtenstein.
- All scheduled SkyItalia flights arriving at an airport within the EU/EEA from a third country, provided the operating carrier is SkyItalia.
- Charter flights operated under the SkyItalia AOC (Air Operator Certificate), when sold as part of a package holiday.

### 1.2 Flights Not Covered

This policy does **not** apply to:

- Passengers travelling free of charge or at a reduced fare not directly or indirectly available to the public (e.g., airline employee travel on staff tickets).
- Passengers on helicopter services.
- Flights to/from territories outside the EU/EEA when operated by a non-EU carrier (not applicable to SkyItalia but noted for completeness regarding codeshare partners).

### 1.3 Ticket and Reservation Requirements

To be eligible for compensation under this policy, passengers must:

1. Hold a confirmed reservation on the disrupted flight.
2. Have presented themselves for check-in at the time indicated in advance by the airline, or — if no specific time was indicated — no later than **45 minutes** before the published departure time (SkyItalia standard check-in deadline for short-haul European flights).
3. For connecting itineraries booked under a single booking reference, have arrived at the connection point on a preceding SkyItalia or partner flight and been unable to make the connection due to the delay or cancellation of the first segment.

### 1.4 SkyItalia Historical Compensation Statistics

In the fiscal year 2025, SkyItalia processed approximately 4,200 compensation claims under Regulation 261/2004, of which 68% were resolved within the 30-day initial response window. The airline paid out a total of approximately EUR 890,000 in statutory compensation, with an additional EUR 310,000 in goodwill gestures (travel vouchers, SkyMiles bonus credits, and cabin upgrades). The most common claim categories were: cancellations (41%), delays exceeding 3 hours (37%), and denied boarding (22%). SkyItalia's internal target for FY2026 is to reduce the average claim resolution time from 22 working days to 15 working days through process automation and enhanced customer service agent training.

## 2. Key Definitions

For the purposes of this document:

- **"Cancellation"** means the non-operation of a flight which was previously planned and on which at least one place was reserved. A flight that departs but returns to the airport of departure without reaching its planned destination is treated as a cancellation. A flight that is diverted to an airport other than the one specified in the itinerary is not treated as a cancellation, but may give rise to delay-based compensation if the diversion results in the passenger arriving at the final destination three or more hours late.

- **"Delay"** means the difference between the scheduled arrival time and the actual arrival time at the final destination. In accordance with the *Sturgeon* ruling, arrival time is defined as the moment when at least one of the aircraft doors is opened, on the assumption that passengers are allowed to leave the aircraft at that point.

- **"Final destination"** means the destination on the ticket presented at the check-in counter, or — in the case of directly connecting flights — the destination of the last flight. Available connecting flights departing before the originally scheduled time are not taken into account.

- **"Denied boarding"** means a refusal to carry passengers on a flight, although they have presented themselves for boarding in accordance with the conditions laid down in Article 3(2) of the Regulation, except where there are reasonable grounds to deny boarding, such as reasons of health, safety or security, or inadequate travel documentation.

- **"Operating air carrier"** means an air carrier that performs or intends to perform a flight under a contract with a passenger or on behalf of another person, legal or natural, having a contract with that passenger. SkyItalia is the operating carrier for all flights bearing the AZ flight number prefix.

- **"Extraordinary circumstances"** — see Section 7 of this document for the complete definition and examples.

- **"Volunteer"** means a person who has presented themselves for boarding and responds positively to the air carrier's call for passengers willing to surrender their reservation in exchange for benefits.

## 3. Compensation Amounts — Article 7(1)

### 3.1 Standard Compensation Table

Under Article 7(1) of Regulation 261/2004, passengers are entitled to the following fixed compensation amounts based on the great-circle distance of the route:

| Distance Category | Great-Circle Distance | Compensation Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Short-haul | Up to 1,500 km | **EUR 250** |
| Medium-haul | 1,500 km to 3,500 km | **EUR 400** |
| Long-haul | Over 3,500 km | **EUR 600** |

**Important note for SkyItalia passengers:** The vast majority of SkyItalia's current route network consists of short-haul European routes with great-circle distances under 1,500 km. This means that the applicable compensation tier for most SkyItalia flights is **EUR 250**. See Section 14 for the complete route distance table.

### 3.2 When Compensation Is Triggered

Passengers become entitled to the compensation amounts listed above when any of the following events occur:

1. **Flight cancellation** — The flight is cancelled and the passenger was not informed at least 14 days before the scheduled departure date (subject to the notice period exceptions detailed in Section 5).

2. **Long delay at arrival** — The flight arrives at the final destination with a delay of **3 hours or more** compared to the originally scheduled arrival time. This principle, established by the CJEU in *Sturgeon v Condor* and confirmed in *Nelson v Lufthansa*, grants delay passengers the same right to compensation as cancellation passengers. The 3-hour threshold is measured at the moment the aircraft doors open at the destination gate.

3. **Denied boarding** — The passenger is involuntarily denied boarding due to overbooking or operational reasons (see Section 9 for the complete denied boarding procedure).

### 3.3 Compensation Currency and Payment Methods

Compensation shall be paid in euro (EUR). SkyItalia offers payment via:

- Bank transfer (SEPA) to any EU/EEA bank account.
- Credit to the original payment method used for the ticket purchase.
- SkyItalia travel vouchers (only with the passenger's **written consent** — the airline may not impose vouchers in lieu of cash).
- SkyMiles loyalty points credit (only with the passenger's written consent, at a conversion rate of 1 EUR = 100 SkyMiles points, e.g., EUR 250 = 25,000 points).

The Regulation requires that compensation is paid "in cash, by electronic bank transfer, bank orders or bank cheques, or — with the signed agreement of the passenger — in travel vouchers and/or other services." SkyItalia's preference is bank transfer, as this provides the clearest audit trail.

## 4. Reduced Compensation — Article 7(2) (The 50% Rule)

### 4.1 Overview

Article 7(2) of Regulation 261/2004 provides that the **compensation may be reduced by 50%** if the airline offers the passenger re-routing to the final destination on an alternative flight, and the arrival time of the alternative flight does not exceed the scheduled arrival time of the originally booked flight by the following thresholds:

| Distance Category | Maximum Excess Arrival Time | Reduced Compensation (50%) |
|---|---|---|
| Up to 1,500 km | **2 hours** | EUR 125 (instead of EUR 250) |
| 1,500 km to 3,500 km | **3 hours** | EUR 200 (instead of EUR 400) |
| Over 3,500 km | **4 hours** | EUR 300 (instead of EUR 600) |

### 4.2 Application to SkyItalia Routes

For all current SkyItalia intra-European routes (which are under 1,500 km), the 50% reduction applies if the alternative flight arrives no more than 2 hours after the originally scheduled arrival time. In practice, this means:

- If your Milan–Rome flight (480 km) is cancelled and SkyItalia rebooks you on a flight that arrives within 2 hours of your original scheduled arrival → compensation is reduced to **EUR 125**.
- If the rebooked flight arrives more than 2 hours after the original scheduled arrival → full compensation of **EUR 250** applies.

### 4.3 Conditions for the 50% Reduction to Apply

The reduced compensation applies only when ALL of the following conditions are met:

1. The airline **actively offers** the alternative routing (the passenger does not have to find it themselves).
2. The alternative flight actually operates and the passenger is transported.
3. The actual arrival time at the final destination does not exceed the thresholds in Section 4.1.
4. The passenger accepts the re-routing offer.

If the alternative flight is itself delayed and the total delay exceeds the threshold, the passenger retains the right to full compensation.

### 4.4 SkyItalia Internal Process Note

Operations controllers should log all re-routing offers in the DCS (Departure Control System) under event code "REROUTE-261" with the alternative flight number, offered time, and passenger acceptance status. This log is essential for defending against subsequent claims where the 50% reduction was legitimately applied. The Customer Relations team will reference the DCS log when processing compensation claims.

## 5. Cancellation — Notice Periods and Exceptions (Article 5)

### 5.1 General Rule

If a flight is cancelled, passengers are entitled to compensation under Article 7 **unless** the airline can prove one of the following:

### 5.2 Notice Period Exceptions

| Notice Given Before Scheduled Departure | Condition for No Compensation | SkyItalia Internal Code |
|---|---|---|
| **14 days or more** | No compensation owed; passenger offered choice of re-routing or refund | CXL-14PLUS |
| **Between 7 and 13 days** | No compensation IF the airline offers re-routing that departs no more than **2 hours before** the scheduled departure AND arrives no more than **4 hours after** the scheduled arrival | CXL-7TO13 |
| **Less than 7 days** | No compensation IF the airline offers re-routing that departs no more than **1 hour before** the scheduled departure AND arrives no more than **2 hours after** the scheduled arrival | CXL-UNDER7 |
| **Less than 7 days** and no suitable alternative offered | **Full compensation** per the distance table in Section 3.1 | CXL-FULL |

### 5.3 Burden of Proof

The burden of proof regarding notification rests with the airline. SkyItalia must be able to demonstrate, through its reservation system records, exactly when the passenger was informed of the cancellation. Notification methods include: email to the address on file, SMS to the phone number on file, push notification via the SkyItalia mobile app, or direct phone call. A notification sent to an invalid or outdated email address does not constitute valid notice, so passengers are encouraged to keep their contact information up to date in their SkyItalia profile.

### 5.4 Early Morning and Late-Night Cancellations

SkyItalia's operations team makes cancellation decisions for the following day's schedule by 21:00 CET the previous evening where possible, to allow time for passenger notification and rebooking. However, operational realities (e.g., aircraft going tech during overnight maintenance, crew falling ill) may result in cancellations being communicated as late as the morning of departure. In such cases, the "less than 7 days" notice period rules apply.

## 6. Long Delay — Arrival Delay of 3 Hours or More

### 6.1 The Sturgeon Principle

While Regulation 261/2004 does not explicitly grant compensation for delays (only for cancellations and denied boarding), the CJEU's *Sturgeon* ruling established that passengers whose flights arrive at the final destination **3 hours or more** after the scheduled arrival time are entitled to compensation under Article 7, by analogy with cancelled flights. This principle was confirmed by the Grand Chamber in *Nelson v Lufthansa* (2012).

### 6.2 Measuring the Delay

The delay is measured as the difference between:

- **Scheduled arrival time**: As printed on the boarding pass or shown in the booking confirmation.
- **Actual arrival time**: The moment when at least one aircraft door is opened at the destination, as established in *Germanwings v Henning* (C-452/13).

Taxiing time after landing is included in the arrival time measurement. Time spent at the gate before the doors open is also included. This matters particularly at large airports like London Heathrow where taxi times can be 15-20 minutes.

### 6.3 Delay Compensation Amounts

The compensation amounts for delays are identical to those for cancellations:

| Distance | Delay Threshold | Compensation |
|---|---|---|
| Up to 1,500 km | Arrival delay of 3+ hours | **EUR 250** |
| 1,500 km to 3,500 km | Arrival delay of 3+ hours | **EUR 400** |
| Over 3,500 km | Arrival delay of 3+ hours | **EUR 600** |

The 50% reduction rule (Section 4) does not typically apply to delay compensation, as there is no "alternative flight" in a delay situation — the passenger is on the same flight, which simply arrives late.

### 6.4 Long Delay Exceeding 5 Hours — Right to Refund

Under Article 6(1)(iii) read in conjunction with Article 8(1)(a), if the delay at departure exceeds **5 hours**, the passenger acquires the same right as a cancellation passenger to abandon the journey and receive a **full refund** of the ticket price within 7 days, including for any unused segments.

Additionally, if the passenger is at a connecting airport and decides to abandon the journey, the airline must provide a return flight to the initial point of departure, at no cost to the passenger.

This is an important distinction: the 3-hour threshold triggers compensation (a fixed payment), while the 5-hour threshold triggers the right to a refund (return of the ticket price). These rights are cumulative — a passenger on a flight delayed by more than 5 hours who abandons the journey may be entitled to **both** a refund of the ticket price and the fixed compensation amount.

### 6.5 SkyItalia Delay Response Protocol

SkyItalia operations controllers classify delays into the following internal categories:

| Delay Duration | Internal Code | Action Triggered |
|---|---|---|
| 15-59 minutes | DEL-MINOR | Gate announcement only |
| 60-119 minutes | DEL-MODERATE | Refreshment vouchers issued at gate |
| 120-179 minutes | DEL-SIGNIFICANT | Full care and assistance activated (Section 8) |
| 180-299 minutes | DEL-MAJOR | Care and assistance + compensation liability noted |
| 300+ minutes | DEL-EXTREME | Care and assistance + refund option offered at gate |

## 7. Extraordinary Circumstances — Article 5(3)

### 7.1 General Principle

The airline is **exempt from paying compensation** if it can prove that the cancellation or delay was caused by **extraordinary circumstances** which could not have been avoided even if all reasonable measures had been taken. The burden of proof lies entirely with the airline.

### 7.2 Events That ARE Extraordinary Circumstances

The following events are recognized by CJEU case law, ENAC guidance, and/or SkyItalia's legal department as constituting extraordinary circumstances. This list is non-exhaustive but represents the most commonly invoked categories:

1. **Severe weather conditions** — Thunderstorms, heavy snowfall, freezing rain, dense fog below operational minimums, microbursts, wind shear events at the destination or departure airport that make safe operations impossible. Ordinary rain or moderate winds within the aircraft's certification envelope are NOT extraordinary.

2. **Volcanic ash clouds** — Following the 2010 Eyjafjallajokull eruption, airspace closures due to volcanic ash are a recognized extraordinary circumstance. SkyItalia monitors VAAC (Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre) bulletins continuously.

3. **Air Traffic Control (ATC) restrictions** — Flow management restrictions imposed by EUROCONTROL or national ATC authorities, ATFM slot delays beyond the airline's control, runway closures, airspace closures for military exercises, ATC system failures, ATC staff shortages due to industrial action.

4. **ATC and airport staff strikes** — Strikes by air traffic controllers, airport ground handling staff, security screeners, or other third parties whose services are essential to the operation of the flight. The key distinction is that these are **third-party** strikes — see Section 7.3 for the airline's own staff.

5. **Bird strikes** — Impact with birds or wildlife that damages the aircraft or requires a mandatory inspection before further service. CJEU case law (*Peskova v Travel Service*, C-315/15) confirms bird strikes as extraordinary, but also requires the airline to show it took reasonable measures (e.g., having a replacement aircraft available where feasible).

6. **Security threats** — Bomb threats, hijack alerts, security breaches at the airport requiring terminal evacuation, unattended baggage alerts that delay boarding, credible terrorism intelligence requiring flight cancellation.

7. **Political instability or civil unrest** — Coups, riots, military conflicts in the airspace or at the destination requiring route changes or cancellations. Overflight bans imposed on specific airspaces (e.g., conflict zones) that make the planned route inoperable.

8. **Medical emergencies on a preceding flight** — If the aircraft operating the passenger's flight was delayed on its inbound leg due to a medical diversion or onboard medical emergency, and no replacement aircraft could be sourced in time.

9. **Airport closures** — Full airport closures due to infrastructure failure (e.g., power outage, runway damage, fire), natural disasters, or security incidents.

10. **Drone sightings** — Unauthorized drone activity in the vicinity of the airport requiring suspension of operations (as occurred at London Gatwick in December 2018).

11. **Lightning strikes on the aircraft** — A direct lightning strike that requires a mandatory unscheduled inspection before the aircraft can return to service.

12. **Hidden manufacturing defects** — Defects in the aircraft that were not discoverable through routine maintenance and inspections, as established in *van der Lans v KLM*. However, the threshold for this defence is very high; the defect must truly be undetectable through normal maintenance programs.

13. **Fuel supply disruptions** — Airport fuel farm failures, contaminated fuel supply, or pipeline breaks that prevent fueling.

14. **Pandemic-related government restrictions** — Government-mandated flight bans, quarantine requirements, or border closures imposed in response to public health emergencies.

15. **Runway incursions or foreign object debris (FOD)** — Debris on the runway causing closure for inspection and clearance.

16. **Sabotage or criminal acts** — Acts of sabotage affecting the aircraft, airport infrastructure, or navigation systems.

17. **Geomagnetic storms or solar flare activity** — In rare cases, severe space weather events can disrupt HF communications and GPS navigation, particularly affecting polar routes.

### 7.3 Events That Are NOT Extraordinary Circumstances

The following events are **NOT** recognized as extraordinary circumstances under CJEU case law and ENAC enforcement practice. When disruptions are caused by these events, SkyItalia **IS** obligated to pay compensation:

1. **Ordinary technical faults** — Most technical problems discovered during pre-flight checks or during operation are considered inherent to the normal exercise of the airline's activity. This includes engine malfunctions, hydraulic leaks, avionics failures, landing gear issues, faulty pressurization systems, and worn brake assemblies — unless they stem from a hidden manufacturing defect (see 7.2.12). The CJEU confirmed in *Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia* that technical problems are not extraordinary merely because they are unexpected.

2. **Routine maintenance failures** — If a technical issue arises because the airline's maintenance program was inadequate, improperly executed, or overdue, this is the airline's responsibility.

3. **Crew scheduling issues** — If a flight is delayed or cancelled because the flight crew exceeded their legal duty time limits (FTL — Flight Time Limitations) and no standby crew was available, this is an operational planning failure, not an extraordinary circumstance. Airlines are expected to have adequate standby crew arrangements.

4. **Crew sickness (foreseeable)** — While a sudden medical incapacitation mid-flight may have elements of extraordinary circumstances, routine crew sick calls that could have been covered by standby rosters are not extraordinary.

5. **Airline staff strikes** — Strikes by the airline's own employees (pilots, cabin crew, ground staff) are **NOT** extraordinary circumstances. The CJEU confirmed in *TUIfly* (C-195/17) that wildcat strikes by the airline's own staff fall within the airline's sphere of responsibility.

6. **IT system failures** — Check-in system outages, reservation system crashes, departure control system failures, and similar IT problems are considered part of the airline's operational responsibility.

7. **Overbooking** — Selling more seats than available is a deliberate commercial practice. Disruptions arising from overbooking are never extraordinary.

8. **Catering delays** — Delays caused by late catering delivery, catering truck breakdown, or incomplete catering loads are operational issues within the airline's control.

9. **Late-arriving inbound aircraft (most cases)** — The aircraft arriving late from a previous flight is generally the airline's operational problem, unless the delay on the previous leg was itself caused by extraordinary circumstances. The so-called "knock-on delay" must be traced back to its root cause to determine if extraordinary circumstances apply.

10. **Baggage loading issues** — Delays caused by baggage system failures, misloaded cargo, or the need to offload baggage of no-show passengers are operational matters.

11. **Fuel calculation errors** — Delays caused by the need for additional fuel uplift due to incorrect flight planning or weight-and-balance errors are not extraordinary.

12. **Aircraft swap resulting in reduced capacity** — If the airline substitutes a smaller aircraft and must deny boarding to some passengers, this is an operational decision, not an extraordinary circumstance.

### 7.4 The "Reasonable Measures" Requirement

Even when extraordinary circumstances exist, the airline must demonstrate it took **all reasonable measures** to minimize the impact. This includes:

- Seeking alternative routing for affected passengers promptly.
- Using spare aircraft from the airline's fleet where available.
- Arranging wet-lease or ACMI capacity from other carriers for prolonged disruptions.
- Cooperating with other airlines to rebook passengers on the earliest available flights.

If the airline fails to take reasonable measures, compensation may still be owed even though the root cause was extraordinary.

## 8. Right to Care and Assistance — Article 9

### 8.1 General Entitlement

Regardless of whether the cause of the disruption constitutes extraordinary circumstances (and therefore regardless of whether compensation is owed), passengers whose flights are delayed or cancelled are entitled to care and assistance from the airline. This right cannot be waived, and it applies from the moment the delay or cancellation becomes apparent.

### 8.2 Care by Delay Duration and Distance

The right to care activates based on the following delay thresholds:

| Distance Category | Delay Threshold for Care | Care Package |
|---|---|---|
| Up to 1,500 km | **2 hours or more** | Meals, refreshments, 2 phone calls/emails/faxes |
| 1,500 km to 3,500 km | **3 hours or more** | Meals, refreshments, 2 phone calls/emails/faxes |
| Over 3,500 km | **4 hours or more** | Meals, refreshments, 2 phone calls/emails/faxes |
| Any distance (overnight) | When departure postponed to next day | Hotel accommodation + transport between airport and hotel |

### 8.3 Specific Care Elements

**Meals and refreshments**: Proportionate to the waiting time. SkyItalia provides refreshment vouchers redeemable at airport food and beverage outlets. Standard voucher values are:

| Waiting Time | Voucher Value |
|---|---|
| 2-4 hours | EUR 8 per passenger |
| 4-8 hours | EUR 15 per passenger (cumulative with first voucher) |
| 8+ hours (daytime) | EUR 25 per passenger (cumulative) |
| Overnight | Hotel accommodation provided (see below) |

**Two phone calls, emails, or faxes**: SkyItalia customer service agents at the gate will facilitate communication for passengers who do not have a mobile phone. In practice, most passengers use their own devices; the airline's obligation is to make the communication possible, not to provide a device.

**Hotel accommodation**: When an overnight stay is necessary, SkyItalia books accommodation at contracted partner hotels near the airport. At Milano Malpensa, the contracted hotels include the Sheraton Milan Malpensa Airport Hotel and the Moxy Milan Malpensa. Passengers may not independently book a luxury hotel and submit the invoice; the accommodation must be "reasonable" and proportionate.

**Transport between airport and hotel**: SkyItalia arranges shuttle bus or taxi service. Passengers who arrange their own transport may submit receipts for reimbursement, subject to a cap of EUR 50 per journey.

### 8.4 Passengers with Reduced Mobility and Unaccompanied Minors

SkyItalia gives particular attention to the needs of passengers with reduced mobility (PRM), unaccompanied minors, and their accompanying persons. These passengers receive priority in care arrangements, including:

- Priority rebooking on the next available flight.
- Assistance with mobility aids and special equipment.
- For unaccompanied minors: direct supervision by a SkyItalia ground staff member at all times until the child is placed on an alternative flight or returned to the care of a parent or guardian.

## 9. Denied Boarding — Overbooking and Volunteer Procedures (Article 4)

### 9.1 Why Overbooking Occurs

Airlines, including SkyItalia, sometimes accept more bookings than there are seats available on a given flight. This practice, known as overbooking, is a standard industry practice designed to account for the statistically predictable number of passengers who will not show up for their flights (no-shows). SkyItalia's revenue management system uses historical data to calculate the optimal overbooking level for each flight, route, and day of week. The target is to maximize aircraft utilization while minimizing the risk of passengers being denied boarding.

### 9.2 Volunteer Solicitation (The "Auction")

When it becomes apparent at the gate that a flight has more passengers than available seats, SkyItalia must **first** call for volunteers willing to surrender their reservation in exchange for benefits. The procedure is as follows:

1. **Gate announcement**: The gate agent announces that the flight is overbooked and asks for volunteers willing to give up their seat.

2. **Initial offer**: SkyItalia makes an initial offer of benefits to volunteers, which may include:
   - Travel vouchers (starting value typically EUR 200-400 depending on the route).
   - Rebooking on the next available SkyItalia flight.
   - Cash compensation.
   - SkyMiles loyalty points bonus.
   - Cabin upgrade on the rebooked flight.
   - Lounge access.

3. **Escalating offers**: If insufficient volunteers come forward at the initial offer level, SkyItalia may increase the offer. There is no regulatory cap on the benefits that can be offered to volunteers — SkyItalia's gate agents have authority to offer up to EUR 500 in vouchers and up to EUR 350 in cash without supervisor approval. Offers above these thresholds require duty manager authorization.

4. **Volunteer agreement**: Volunteers who accept an offer sign a voluntary denied boarding form (VDB-FORM-01) and receive a written confirmation of the agreed benefits. Volunteers retain their right to choose between re-routing and a full refund for their original ticket, in addition to the volunteered benefits.

### 9.3 Involuntary Denied Boarding

If, after calling for volunteers, there are still not enough seats available, the airline must deny boarding to passengers **involuntarily**. SkyItalia's boarding priority criteria for determining which passengers are denied boarding (in order of decreasing protection) are:

1. Passengers with reduced mobility and their accompanying persons.
2. Unaccompanied minors.
3. Passengers on connecting flights with the tightest connections.
4. SkyMiles Platinum and Gold members.
5. Business class passengers.
6. Passengers who checked in earliest.
7. All other passengers (last to check in = first to be denied boarding).

### 9.4 Rights of Involuntarily Denied Passengers

Passengers who are involuntarily denied boarding are entitled to **all of the following**, regardless of the reason for overbooking and without any extraordinary circumstances defence:

1. **Full monetary compensation** per the distance table in Section 3.1 (EUR 250 / EUR 400 / EUR 600).
2. **Choice of re-routing or full refund** (same as cancellation — see Section 5).
3. **Care and assistance** while waiting for the rebooked flight (Section 8).

The airline **cannot** invoke extraordinary circumstances to deny compensation for overbooking. Denied boarding compensation is always payable.

### 9.5 SkyItalia Denied Boarding Statistics

In 2025, SkyItalia denied boarding to approximately 320 passengers across its network, of which 280 were voluntary (accepted compensation offers) and 40 were involuntary. The average voluntary denied boarding compensation was EUR 285 (a mix of vouchers, cash, and points). Involuntary denied boarding events were concentrated on the Milan-Rome route during peak business travel periods (Monday mornings and Friday evenings).

## 10. Involuntary Downgrade — Article 10

### 10.1 When Downgrade Compensation Applies

If a passenger is placed in a class lower than that for which the ticket was purchased (e.g., booked in Business but seated in Economy due to aircraft swap), the airline must reimburse a percentage of the ticket price within **7 days**:

| Distance Category | Reimbursement Percentage |
|---|---|
| Up to 1,500 km | **30%** of the ticket price |
| 1,500 km to 3,500 km | **50%** of the ticket price |
| Over 3,500 km | **75%** of the ticket price |

### 10.2 Calculation Basis

The reimbursement is calculated on the **full ticket price** of the affected flight leg, including taxes and surcharges. If the ticket was purchased as part of a round-trip or multi-city itinerary, the reimbursement applies only to the proportional price of the affected leg. SkyItalia's revenue accounting department uses the IATA proration formula to determine the per-leg price of multi-leg tickets.

### 10.3 Voluntary Upgrade

If a passenger is placed in a class **higher** than that for which the ticket was purchased (an upgrade), no additional payment is due from the passenger. SkyItalia may upgrade passengers at its discretion, particularly SkyMiles loyalty program members, and such upgrades do not create any obligation for the passenger.

### 10.4 SkyItalia Aircraft Swap Scenarios

Downgrade situations most commonly arise when SkyItalia substitutes a different aircraft type on a route. For example, if a flight normally operated by an Airbus A321neo (which has a Business class cabin) is substituted by a Boeing 737-800 (which on SkyItalia's configuration does not have a separate Business class cabin), Business class passengers may need to be downgraded to Economy. In such cases, the Article 10 reimbursement applies automatically, and SkyItalia's operations department will flag the affected PNRs (Passenger Name Records) for proactive outreach by the Customer Relations team.

## 11. Connecting Flights

### 11.1 Single Booking Reference

When a passenger holds a booking with connecting flights under a **single booking reference** (e.g., Milan → Rome → Catania booked as a single itinerary), the compensation is calculated based on:

- The **total great-circle distance** from the point of origin to the final destination.
- The **delay at the final destination**, not at the connection point.

This means that if the first leg (Milan → Rome) is delayed by 1 hour and the passenger misses their connection to Catania, resulting in a total delay of 4 hours at Catania, the passenger is entitled to compensation based on the total Milan–Catania distance and the 4-hour delay.

### 11.2 Separate Bookings

If the passenger booked two separate tickets (e.g., one ticket Milan → Rome, another ticket Rome → Catania), the flights are treated independently. A delay on the first flight does not create any obligation on the airline operating the second flight. Passengers are strongly advised to book connecting itineraries under a single booking reference to ensure full protection.

### 11.3 SkyItalia Connection Guarantee

SkyItalia offers a "Connection Guarantee" for all itineraries booked under a single PNR through SkyItalia channels (website, app, or call center). Under this guarantee, if SkyItalia is responsible for a missed connection (whether due to delay, cancellation, or schedule change on the first leg), the airline will:

- Rebook the passenger on the next available flight at no extra cost.
- Provide care and assistance during the waiting period.
- Honor any compensation entitlement based on the total itinerary distance and arrival delay at the final destination.

## 12. SkyItalia Proactive Compensation Policy

### 12.1 Automatic Compensation for Short-Haul Delays

In addition to the statutory requirements of Regulation 261/2004, SkyItalia has implemented a **proactive compensation program** for delays exceeding 3 hours on routes under 1,500 km (which covers the majority of the SkyItalia network).

Under this program:

- When a SkyItalia flight on a route under 1,500 km arrives at its destination with a delay of 3 hours or more, the airline's automated system (DCS integration with the Customer Relations CRM) **automatically** generates a compensation notification to all affected passengers within 48 hours.
- The notification includes the compensation amount (EUR 250), a link to the online claim form with pre-filled flight and passenger details, and the option to receive payment via bank transfer or SkyMiles points.
- Passengers who accept proactive compensation waive their right to pursue the same claim through ENAC or the courts. However, they retain the right to claim for additional damages (e.g., consequential losses such as missed hotel bookings or event tickets) through the normal complaints procedure.

### 12.2 Goodwill Gestures

SkyItalia also has a discretionary goodwill gesture program for delays between 1 and 3 hours, where no statutory compensation is owed. These gestures may include:

- SkyMiles bonus points (typically 500-2,000 points depending on the delay duration and the passenger's loyalty tier).
- Travel vouchers of EUR 20-50.
- Complimentary cabin upgrade on a future flight (subject to availability).

These goodwill gestures are at the sole discretion of SkyItalia and do not create any precedent or obligation.

### 12.3 Peak Season Disruption Protocol

During peak travel periods (Christmas/New Year, Easter, August), SkyItalia pre-positions additional customer service agents at major airports and activates a dedicated disruption management team in the airline's Operations Control Centre (OCC) in Milan. This team has authority to charter additional capacity, arrange hotel blocks, and deploy mobile customer service desks at gates experiencing significant delays.

## 13. Complaint Procedure and Legal Remedies

### 13.1 Step 1: Complaint to SkyItalia

Passengers must first submit their complaint directly to SkyItalia. Complaints can be filed through:

- **Online form**: Available at www.skyitalia.com/complaints (preferred method for fastest processing).
- **Email**: complaints@skyitalia.com.
- **Post**: SkyItalia Customer Relations Department, Aeroporto di Milano Malpensa, Terminal 1, 21010 Ferno (VA), Italy.
- **Phone**: SkyItalia Customer Service at +39 02 9999 0000 (note: phone complaints should be followed up in writing for documentation purposes).

**Required information for a compensation claim:**
- Passenger full name (as it appears on the booking).
- Booking reference number.
- Flight number and date.
- Description of the disruption experienced.
- Preferred compensation payment method.
- Copy of boarding pass or booking confirmation (recommended but not mandatory for SkyItalia passengers, as the airline can verify the booking internally).

**SkyItalia response timeline:** SkyItalia aims to provide a substantive response within **30 days** of receiving a complete claim. For complex cases (e.g., those involving extraordinary circumstances disputes, connecting flight calculations, or third-party involvement), the response time may extend to **6 weeks** (42 days). If the airline fails to respond within 6 weeks, the passenger may proceed to Step 2.

### 13.2 Step 2: Escalation to ENAC (National Enforcement Body)

If the passenger is unsatisfied with SkyItalia's response, or if SkyItalia fails to respond within 6 weeks, the passenger may file a complaint with:

**ENAC — Ente Nazionale per l'Aviazione Civile**
Viale del Castro Pretorio, 118
00185 Roma, Italy
Website: www.enac.gov.it
Online complaint form: Available on the ENAC website under "Diritti dei Passeggeri"

ENAC is Italy's designated national enforcement body under Article 16 of Regulation 261/2004. ENAC has the power to:

- Investigate airlines operating from Italian airports.
- Impose administrative sanctions on airlines that systematically fail to comply with the Regulation.
- Mediate between passengers and airlines in individual cases (though ENAC cannot order compensation payments directly).

For flights departing from airports in other EU member states, the complaint should be filed with the enforcement body of that member state (e.g., CAA for the UK, DGAC for France, AESA for Spain).

### 13.3 Step 3: ADR (Alternative Dispute Resolution) / Conciliation

If the ENAC process does not resolve the dispute, passengers may pursue Alternative Dispute Resolution. In Italy, the relevant ADR bodies for airline passenger disputes include:

- **Conciliazione paritetica** — A voluntary conciliation process between consumer associations and the airline. SkyItalia participates in the ADR scheme administered by the Camera di Commercio di Milano.
- **European Consumer Centre (ECC-Net)** — For cross-border disputes involving passengers from other EU member states.
- **Online Dispute Resolution (ODR) platform** — The EU's ODR platform (ec.europa.eu/odr) is available for online purchases of flight tickets.

### 13.4 Step 4: Small Claims Court (Giudice di Pace)

As a last resort, passengers may file a claim before the **Giudice di Pace** (Justice of the Peace) for claims up to EUR 5,000, or the **Tribunale Ordinario** (Ordinary Court) for claims above EUR 5,000. Given that individual compensation claims under Regulation 261/2004 are EUR 250, EUR 400, or EUR 600, most claims fall within the Giudice di Pace's jurisdiction.

Key considerations for court proceedings:

- **Statute of limitations**: In Italy, the limitation period for claims under Regulation 261/2004 is **2 years** from the date of the flight, in accordance with Article 2947 of the Italian Civil Code (short prescription for tort claims). Some other EU member states apply longer limitation periods (e.g., 6 years in the UK, 3 years in Germany).
- **Court fees**: Filing fees before the Giudice di Pace for claims up to EUR 1,100 are EUR 43. For claims between EUR 1,100 and EUR 5,000, the filing fee is EUR 98.
- **Legal representation**: Representation by a lawyer is not mandatory before the Giudice di Pace for claims up to EUR 1,100. For higher amounts, legal representation is required.
- **Competent court**: The claim may be filed at the court of the passenger's place of residence, the airline's registered office, or the departure/arrival airport.

### 13.5 Third-Party Claim Companies

Passengers may choose to use third-party claim management companies (e.g., AirHelp, Flightright, Compensair) that pursue compensation claims on the passenger's behalf in exchange for a commission (typically 25-35% of the compensation amount). SkyItalia neither endorses nor discourages the use of such services, but notes that passengers can file claims directly with the airline at no cost. Claims submitted through third-party companies are processed with the same timeline and criteria as direct claims.

## 15. Interaction with the Montreal Convention and Additional Claims

### 15.1 Regulation 261/2004 vs. Montreal Convention

Regulation 261/2004 provides **fixed, standardized compensation** that does not require the passenger to prove actual loss. The Montreal Convention (1999), transposed into EU law by Regulation (EC) No 889/2002, provides for compensation of **proven actual damages** up to approximately SDR 5,346 (approximately EUR 6,300) for delays.

These two regimes are complementary, not mutually exclusive. A passenger may claim the fixed EUR 250 under Regulation 261/2004 **and** pursue a Montreal Convention claim for additional proven damages (e.g., a missed non-refundable hotel booking, a missed business meeting with demonstrable financial loss). However, the fixed compensation received under Regulation 261/2004 may be deducted from any Montreal Convention damages award, to avoid double recovery.

### 15.2 Consequential Damages

SkyItalia's liability for consequential damages beyond the fixed statutory compensation is governed by the Montreal Convention and Italian law. Passengers seeking to claim consequential damages should:

1. Document all losses with receipts, invoices, and written evidence.
2. Demonstrate causation between the flight disruption and the financial loss.
3. Demonstrate that the losses were foreseeable and reasonable.
4. Submit the claim to SkyItalia Customer Relations with supporting documentation.

## 16. Special Categories and Additional Provisions

### 16.1 Passengers with Disabilities and Reduced Mobility

Under Regulation (EC) No 1107/2006, passengers with disabilities and reduced mobility enjoy enhanced protection during disruptions. SkyItalia ensures that:

- Wheelchair users receive priority rebooking.
- Passengers travelling with medical equipment are accommodated on alternative flights with appropriate storage.
- Assistance dogs are accepted on alternative flights without additional charge or delay.
- Communication regarding delays and cancellations is provided in accessible formats upon request.

### 16.2 Group Bookings

Groups of 10 or more passengers travelling under a single group PNR are entitled to the same individual compensation rights as individual passengers. Each member of the group may file a separate claim or the group leader may file on behalf of all members with appropriate written authorization.

### 16.3 Infants and Children

Children travelling on a ticket (regardless of fare paid) are entitled to full compensation under Regulation 261/2004. Infants travelling on a parent's lap without a separate seat and without a separate ticket are not entitled to compensation, as they do not hold a confirmed reservation for a seat.

## 18. Frequently Asked Questions — Compensation

**Q: My flight was delayed by 2 hours and 50 minutes. Am I entitled to compensation?**
A: No. Compensation under Regulation 261/2004 is triggered only when the arrival delay is **3 hours or more**. A delay of 2 hours and 50 minutes, while inconvenient, does not reach the threshold. However, you may be entitled to care and assistance (meals, refreshments) if the delay at departure exceeded 2 hours for flights under 1,500 km.

**Q: My flight was cancelled due to bad weather, but I had to pay for a hotel myself because the airline said they couldn't arrange one. Can I get reimbursed?**
A: Yes. Even when extraordinary circumstances apply (meaning no compensation is owed), the airline's duty to provide care and assistance — including hotel accommodation — remains. If SkyItalia was unable to arrange accommodation and you booked it yourself, submit the receipt to SkyItalia Customer Relations for reimbursement. Keep the receipt reasonable and proportionate.

**Q: I was on a business trip and the flight delay caused me to miss an important meeting, resulting in financial loss. Can I claim more than EUR 250?**
A: The EUR 250 under Regulation 261/2004 is a fixed, automatic payment. For additional provable losses, you may file a claim under the Montreal Convention. You would need to provide documentation of the financial loss and demonstrate the causal link to the flight disruption.

**Q: The airline says my delay was due to "operational reasons." Does that count as extraordinary circumstances?**
A: No. "Operational reasons" is a vague term that typically covers scheduling, crew availability, or aircraft rotation issues. These are within the airline's control and are not extraordinary circumstances.

**Q: I missed my connecting flight because the first leg was delayed. Who is responsible?**
A: If both flights were booked under a single booking reference, SkyItalia is responsible for rebooking you and, if the delay at your final destination is 3+ hours, paying compensation based on the total distance. If the flights were on separate bookings, the airline operating the delayed first leg has no obligation regarding the missed connection.

**Q: Can the airline force me to accept a travel voucher instead of cash?**
A: No. Under Regulation 261/2004, compensation must be paid in cash (or bank transfer) unless the passenger gives **signed written consent** to accept vouchers instead. You have the right to insist on monetary payment.
